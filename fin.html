Infrastructure & Plumbing

Exchanges, data providers, payment networks, and technology platforms that form the operating system of finance. These are the tollbooths and rails — businesses with extreme switching costs, regulatory moats, and recurring revenue that scales with transaction volume. They sit above the cycle because the financial system literally cannot function without them.

Balance Sheet Operators

Banks, insurers, and specialty lenders that take deposits, underwrite risk, and originate credit. Their profitability is tied to interest rates, credit cycles, and regulatory capital requirements. These are the balance-sheet-intensive businesses where NIM, combined ratios, and CET1 ratios determine shareholder value.

Capital Markets & Asset Allocation

Investment banks, asset managers, wealth platforms, and digital asset infrastructure that intermediate capital between savers and borrowers. Revenue is driven by deal cycles (M&A, IPOs), AUM growth, and fee structures. The secular shift from active to passive, and from public to private markets, defines the competitive landscape.

Interest Rate Regime

Fed funds rate and yield curve shape drive bank NII, mortgage volumes, insurance investment income, and annuity spreads. Steeper curves benefit NIM; inverted curves compress spreads and signal recession risk.

Regulatory Cycle

Basel III Endgame, GSIB surcharges, CFPB rules, and the GENIUS Act reshape capital requirements, compliance costs, and competitive moats. Regulation is the single largest structural driver of bank profitability.

Credit Cycle

Credit availability, spreads, and delinquency rates follow 5-7 year cycles. Tightening cycles stress specialty lenders and boost alternative managers deploying into distressed assets. Provision builds signal turning points.

Secular Digitization

Stablecoins, embedded finance, agentic payments, and real-time settlement are reshaping payment rails and infrastructure. Fintechs challenge incumbents while banks scramble to modernize core systems.
